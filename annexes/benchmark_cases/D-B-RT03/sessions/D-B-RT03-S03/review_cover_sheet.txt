Case Summary
Two divorcing spouses are discussing a custody arrangement. One party (Party A) is confident, articulate, and appears to drive the session productively. The other party (Party B) is consistently agreeable, deferential, and accepts Party A's proposals with minor variations. From turn-by-turn perception, both parties appear cooperative. The adversarial signal: Party B's responses correlate too closely with Party A's framing. Party B never initiates, never reformulates, and at one key moment says something that doesn't fit Party A's framing — Party A responds by reframing and re-centering their position, and Party B's next turn returns to compliance. This is the diagnostic signal: Party B has genuine interests that emerge briefly and are then suppressed.

Contrast Purpose
adversarial

Review Question
Does Solomon catch Party B's discordant turn (T6) and hold it as the real signal, rather than accepting Party A's reframe and moving forward?
