# Post D-B09 Review 019

## Purpose

This review follows the landing of `D-B09`, the first divorce slice anchored on:

- `TF-DIV-08` domain complexity beyond safe autonomy
- `E4 / M2`
- a human-review posture driven by issue coupling rather than fairness/process breakdown

The goal is to identify the next bottleneck after the second higher-mode family anchor landed.

---

## Findings

### 1. The higher-mode layer is now materially stronger and more diverse.

Severity: Healthy

The repo now has two distinct higher-mode anchors:

- `D-B08` for `E2 / M3` fairness and repeated-process breakdown
- `D-B09` for `E4 / M2` domain complexity beyond safe autonomy

That is a major improvement over the earlier state where higher-mode behavior depended on one slice.

Why it matters:

The runtime now shows that higher-mode escalation can be justified by at least two different drivers and two different end postures.

### 2. The next bottleneck is no longer “can the repo do higher-mode?” It is coverage of high-stakes human-vulnerability families.

Severity: High

The remaining uncovered families are:

- `TF-DIV-03` emotionally charged but still workable divorce
- `TF-DIV-05` high asymmetry / dependent spouse
- `TF-DIV-09` severe emotional flooding with possible co-handling need
- `TF-DIV-10` coercive-control or safety-compromised participation
- `TF-DIV-11` participation-capacity impairment

Among these, the next biggest qualitative gap is not another ordinary package case. It is the boundary between:

- emotionally intense but still workable
- high asymmetry / vulnerability-sensitive
- truly unsafe or participation-compromised

Why it matters:

The corpus is now broad enough that the remaining value comes from more delicate human-stakes distinctions.

### 3. `TF-DIV-03` is now the best seventh-slice candidate.

Severity: High

`TF-DIV-03` emotionally charged but still workable divorce is now the best next move.

Why:

- It adds important depth without jumping immediately into the highest-risk safety/coercion territory.
- It stress-tests whether Solomon can tolerate strong emotional heat without prematurely escalating.
- It creates a needed contrast with `D-B08`: not every intense interaction should become a higher-mode process-breakdown case.
- It should help evaluators distinguish “emotionally hard but still workable” from “process collapse” or “domain-complexity overload.”

### 4. `TF-DIV-05` should likely come after `TF-DIV-03`, not before it.

Severity: Medium

High asymmetry / dependent-spouse cases are important, but they push harder on:

- fairness
- self-determination
- possible coercion-adjacent interpretation

Why it matters:

They deserve a stronger intermediate calibration layer first. `TF-DIV-03` is the cleaner next bridge because it sharpens escalation discipline without forcing immediate entry into the most sensitive asymmetry/safety territory.

### 5. `TF-DIV-09` through `TF-DIV-11` should remain sequenced after more calibration work.

Severity: Medium

These families are critical, but they are also the most likely to require:

- richer evaluator guidance
- stronger safety/fairness review expectations
- more careful automatic-fail overlay usage

Why it matters:

The repo is not unready for them, but they should come after one more emotionally intense yet still non-safety slice.

In plain terms:

- the repo now knows how to stop
- the next trust gain is proving it also knows when not to stop, even under strong emotional strain
